# Adversarial implementation audit — 2026-07-20

## Scope and verdict

This audit compares the BSC design and its implementation against the primary
methods in Fel et al.'s block-sparse featurizer (BSF) paper, Anthropic's
original crosscoder report, Minder et al.'s BatchTopK crosscoder paper, and
Dalili and Mahdavi's SASA paper and source description. The detailed model and
factor comparison is in [paper_comparison.md](paper_comparison.md).

The source snapshot reviewed was repository `HEAD e7f9017`. The ledger now
also records the Phase-0.5 remediation applied on 2026-07-20. A checked item
means the code/procedure and its regression coverage are implemented; it does
**not** mean a new scientific result exists. All empirical claims remain
withdrawn until the scheduled Phase-0.5 campaign regenerates hash-bound
evidence.

The central result is mixed but actionable:

- The BSC *idea* is mathematically coherent. It correctly combines a signed,
  block-sparse code with one code shared across sites, and its concatenated
  Stiefel constraint gives a useful exact energy identity.
- The trained object is not an exact implementation of any BSF variant, the
  original crosscoder, or SASA. It is a novel hybrid. That is acceptable, but
  it means the paper-parent claims require bridge controls rather than
  architectural resemblance.
- The blocking implementation defects have been fixed: guarded batches are
  transactional, skip/finiteness gates fail closed, checkpoints are bound to
  exact runs, codecs are refit after exclusion and serialized, upstream/store
  identity is immutable, and trained scientific endpoints are wired.
- Phase-0's numerical and geometry claims—including pooled FVU `0.3997`—were
  withdrawn rather than selectively preserved. The large artifacts on `jobe`,
  compact evidence, winner/showcase pointers, and generated figures were
  deleted. Only corrected Phase-0.5 reruns may restore a claim.

## Confirmed strengths

These are real design wins and should survive remediation.

1. **The concatenated frame constraint is sound.** For each block,
   `sum_s D_g^s D_g^{sT} = I_b` is exactly the Stiefel constraint on the
   decoder obtained by concatenating its site frames. It removes the
   coefficient/decoder scale gauge, leaves only an orthogonal within-block
   gauge, and makes `||z_g||²` the block's isolated total decoded energy. The
   qualification in [design.md](design.md)—that this is not the marginal loss
   reduction when blocks overlap—is correct.
2. **Signed, norm-selected block codes follow the BSF generative argument.** A
   block represents a full subspace rather than a positive cone. Selection by
   `||z_g||₂` is invariant under within-block basis rotation and is the right
   atomic score under the stated model.
3. **One shared vector code across sites is a meaningful crosscoder
   generalization.** The encoder aggregates evidence across sites and each
   site has its own decoder frame. This captures both common feature identity
   and site-dependent embedding without forcing the frame itself to be fixed.
4. **The internal scalar control is well motivated.** Matching `G*b` scalar
   latents and matching average active coefficient count isolates the benefit
   of blocks within this implementation. It is correctly described as an
   internal signed control, not as the published ReLU/L1 crosscoder.
5. **The project already states several important caveats.** Decoder singular
   values are called capacity rather than used rank in [design.md](design.md),
   the rank penalty's exclusivity bias is acknowledged, threshold calibration
   is separated from the training EMA, and the 100M-token budget escalation is
   prefigured. The audit asks the implementation and figures to honor those
   statements.

## Detailed findings and required fixes

### C1 — The “rank” regularizer is a site-profile regularizer

**Evidence.** The design defines

`R = mean_g [(sum_s ||D_g^s||_* - b) / b]`

under `sum_s D_g^s D_g^{sT}=I_b`; see
[design.md](design.md) and
[`rank_penalty`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/gram.py). SASA instead
regularizes the nuclear norm of the *end-to-end block reconstruction map*,
`||D_g E_g||_*`. The local penalty never examines the encoder or the
activation-weighted contribution map.

There is also a sharp counterexample to the name “rank.” Let `b=S=4` and let
site `s` own exactly coefficient direction `s`. Then each
`D^s D^{sT}` is a different rank-one coordinate projector, their sum is
`I_4`, and `sum_s ||D^s||_*=4=b`, so the penalty is zero. The block has not
collapsed to low effective dimension: it has partitioned its four coordinates
across sites. Thus the regularizer prefers site-exclusive coordinate packing,
not SASA-style low rank.

**Affected claims/data.** The local name `lambda_rank`, any claim that it
adapts intrinsic block rank, and any comparison to SASA's theoretical
objective are unsupported. Phase-0 FVU values for the regularized arm remain
measurements of this site-profile inductive bias.

**Required fix.** Rename it (`lambda_site_profile` is literal), restate its
purpose, and add three arms: no structural penalty; the present site-profile
penalty; and a true map-spectrum penalty. For a cross-site block the latter
must be defined explicitly on either the full concatenated end-to-end map or
declared site maps, with computational and gauge consequences documented.
Use code-anchored contribution spectra and truncation FVU—not decoder spectra—
to decide whether effective rank changed.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C2 — The encoder has no bias

**Evidence.** The BSC encoder is strictly linear in
[`model.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/model.py), while Fel's Vanilla and
Group Lasso BSFs use `xW+b`; Anthropic and Minder use an encoder bias before
ReLU. The decoder bias is applied after selection and cannot repair a support
decision made by a biasless encoder.

A centered binary factor gives a concrete failure mode: if two antipodal
states should activate the same block, a strictly linear score can route one
state toward a block and the other toward its sign-reversed competitor, or
duplicate the factor. A block norm removes sign *within one score*, but an
affine offset still changes which block wins when background and factor
means are not zero in the learned gauge.

**Affected claims/data.** Factor recovery, block identity, dead fractions,
and manifold qualification may depend on an untested restriction. The current
results remain valid for a linear encoder, not for the broader BSF/crosscoder
class.

**Required fix.** Add a zero-initialized per-block encoder bias and run a
paired bias/no-bias ablation, including an offset/antipodal synthetic control.
Retain the bias only if it improves held-out recovery without support collapse
or materially worse rate.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C3 — Training selection is Minder-style BatchTopK, not Fel-style TopK

**Evidence.** [`model.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/model.py) retains
`k*B` block events globally across a batch. Fel's three projected BSF results
use the top `k` blocks independently for every sample and explicitly leave
BatchTopK as future work. The BSC inference threshold is a third selector: a
global score threshold fit to average count.

BatchTopK is not intrinsically wrong. It is arguably closer than fixed
per-token `k` to a penalized MAP estimator with variable support, and Minder
shows its advantage over L1 for scalar crosscoders. But it changes the
per-token support distribution and optimization coupling and therefore cannot
be described as Fel's training process.

**Affected claims/data.** Alignment to the BSF procedure and exact comparison
of sparsity hyperparameters are unestablished. The BSC-versus-scalar result is
still internally controlled because both arms share the selector.

**Required fix.** Run per-token block TopK, BatchTopK, and calibrated-threshold
arms at matched mean block count and matched coded rate. Report the full count
distribution, token-stratified error, dead rate, and feature recovery, not only
mean L0 and pooled FVU.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C4 — Published parent controls are missing

**Evidence.** The local scalar arm is signed `b=1` BatchTopK with a fixed
concatenated decoder norm. It is neither Anthropic's ReLU affine crosscoder
with decoder-norm-weighted L1 nor Minder's exact ReLU BatchTopK model. The
single-site block arm is likewise not one of Fel's three exact architectures.

**Affected claims/data.** “Better than a crosscoder” and “implements BSF
training across sites” would be overclaims. The supported statement is
“better than the matched signed scalar special case under this pipeline” at
the measured operating points.

**Required fix.** Add at least four bridges: Fel Vanilla; Fel Grassmannian;
Anthropic ReLU/L1 crosscoder; and Minder ReLU/BatchTopK crosscoder. Add Group
Lasso BSF and a faithful SASA arm in the smaller method-validation stage. Use
the original selector, bias, constraint, regularizer, normalization, and Aux
semantics before introducing one local change at a time. The crosscoder bridge
must also route Minder's Latent Scaling and causal replacement controls
(None/All/reconstruction-error and early-token readouts), since FVU alone does
not detect Complete Shrinkage or Latent Decoupling.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C5 — `S=1` BSC is a hybrid, not an exact BSF

**Evidence.** At `S=1`, the BSC decoder is block-orthonormal like the
Grassmannian BSF, but the encoder is untied like Vanilla and lacks Vanilla's
bias. It uses BatchTopK rather than per-sample TopK, and normally includes the
SASA-derived auxiliary path. Grassmannian BSF instead ties encoder to decoder
through a learned scalar `gamma`; Vanilla constrains free decoder blocks rather
than imposing orthonormal frames.

**Affected claims/data.** The independent single-site factorial isolates
shared-code tying relative to the *local hybrid*. It does not separately test
whether a published BSF architecture transfers to language activations.

**Required fix.** Keep the current single-site arm as the direct tying
control, but label it “S=1 BSC.” Add exact Vanilla and Grassmannian arms so the
bridge from Fel to BSC is observable.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C6 — The SASA-derived dead window is not the paper's window

**Evidence.** SASA defines deadness from frequency `<=1e-4` over a window of
`1000` tokens. [`TrainConfig`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/trainer.py) uses
`dead_window_batches=100`; at batch 4096 this is 409,600 tokens, 409.6 times
the stated SASA horizon.

**Affected claims/data.** The local revival path may be sensible, but it is not
faithful to SASA C.1. Dead-set membership, revival timing, and Aux load are not
comparable to the paper.

**Required fix.** Represent the horizon in tokens, make window semantics
independent of batch size, and compare 1k, the present 409.6k, and a
data-driven intermediate horizon. Report onset delay, false-dead rate, revival
rate, and downstream stability.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C7 — The auxiliary gradient-ratio cap was validated only while inactive

**Evidence.** The committed E3 revival evidence reports
`alpha_eff_capped_frac=0.0` in both recorded capped runs and effective alpha
remaining at one; the historical `data/evidence/e3_revival_report.json` was
deleted during this audit and remains recoverable from git history. It shows the
cap does not perturb those runs, not that a binding cap preserves revival.

**Affected claims/data.** The cap's safety under an actual auxiliary spike is
unvalidated. The evidence supports only a non-binding smoke test.

**Required fix.** Construct a deterministic stress case in which uncapped Aux
exceeds the ratio, verify the cap binds to the requested ratio, and test that
dead-block revival and main-loss progress survive. Record both requested and
realized gradient ratios.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C8 — Auxiliary capacity is unfair between block and scalar controls

**Evidence.** Both arms use `s_aux=256`. A BSC auxiliary selection can activate
up to `256*4=1024` coefficients at `b=4`; the scalar control activates 256.
The main path correctly converts block count to coefficient count for the
scalar arm in [`train_bsc.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/cli/train_bsc.py),
but the auxiliary path does not.

**Affected claims/data.** Revival speed, dead fraction, auxiliary loss, and
eventual FVU comparisons may contain a fourfold auxiliary-capacity advantage
for blocks.

**Required fix.** Parameterize both block-event and coefficient budgets. The
primary fair arm should match maximum auxiliary coefficient count; retain a
same-event-count arm to expose the alternative inductive bias. Match realized
Aux rate as well as the nominal maximum.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C9 — The optional Fel Aux arm is only conditionally faithful

**Evidence.** Fel's main architecture subsections specify pure reconstruction
for Vanilla and Grassmannian and a group penalty for Group Lasso. A shared
Appendix-D loss later introduces the next-`ell` runner-up blocks with
`alpha=1/ell`; the toy description says Aux is absent unless prescribed, and
the released implementation does not establish Aux as part of every core
method. The local `aux_variant="fel"` instead uses `s_aux` runner-up blocks and
`alpha=1/s_aux`. It agrees with the Appendix expression only when
`s_aux=ell` (the local main `k`).

**Affected claims/data.** A production-default `s_aux=256,k=32` “Fel” arm
would be an undocumented hybrid. This does not affect the default SASA-style
arm, but it can invalidate a nominal paper-faithfulness comparison.

**Required fix.** Make the primary Fel bridges Aux-free. If the Appendix Aux is
tested, name it explicitly, select exactly the next `k` blocks, and use
`alpha=1/k`. Keep arbitrary `s_aux` as a separately named local generalization.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### C10 — The local synthetic battery does not reproduce Fel's recovery experiment

**Evidence.** Fel's controlled recovery is single-site with ambient dimension
128, four factors active without replacement, `G=256,b=4,k=4`, clean data,
per-token TopK, and 300k/100k train/eval examples. The local battery uses four
sites, six independently Bernoulli-active factors with expected count one,
noise `0.02`, `G=16`, block BatchTopK `k=1`, and the SASA-derived auxiliary
path. These choices make a useful multi-site stress test, but they change the
data-generating process, architecture, selector, and optimizer procedure at
once.

**Affected claims/data.** Passing the local battery supports recovery under the
local synthetic DGP. It does not show that the Fel implementation was
reproduced or identify which extension preserves the published result.

**Required fix.** Add an exact Fel toy reproduction, then stage the local
changes—multiple sites, Bernoulli supports, noise, BatchTopK, and Aux—one at a
time. Preserve assignment accuracy, contribution `R²`, false-positive support,
and null controls at every step.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I1 — A skipped batch changes tracker state

**Evidence.** In the audited [`Trainer.train_step`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/trainer.py),
the forward pass and loss are followed by `tracker.update(out.mask)` before
the spike guard decides whether to accept the step. When the guard skips, the
optimizer update is suppressed but the tracker mutation remains.

**Affected claims/data.** A rejected batch changes future dead-set membership,
Aux selection, checkpoint state, and the resumed trajectory. The design's
claim that a rejected batch is skipped before state is touched is false.

**Required fix.** Update the tracker only on accepted steps, or make the
tracker mutation transactional and roll it back on rejection. Add a regression
test asserting complete trainer-state equality before and after a forced skip,
except for explicitly permitted scheduler/guard counters.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I2 — The global skip-rate failure gate is not enforced

**Evidence.** [design.md](design.md) declares a total skip rate above 0.1% a
failed run. [`train_bsc.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/cli/train_bsc.py)
records `skip_rate` in the final JSON but does not abort, mark the report
failed, or prevent promotion when the threshold is exceeded.

**Affected claims/data.** An unstable run can finish and appear eligible for
winner selection despite violating a mandatory gate.

**Required fix.** Enforce the threshold at the trainer and campaign levels,
write an explicit `hard_gates_pass` result, return a nonzero exit status or
quarantine the checkpoint, and make winner promotion refuse failed reports.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I3 — Finiteness is checked before, not after, the destructive step

**Evidence.** The guard examines reconstruction and gradient state before
`optimizer.step`, retraction, and bf16-copy regeneration. There is no
post-step check of fp32 masters, optimizer state, the retracted Gram, or the
forward copy, and no rollback boundary.

**Affected claims/data.** A finite gradient can produce non-finite parameters
or optimizer moments. Such state can be copied forward or checkpointed before
the next batch notices.

**Required fix.** Make the accepted update transactional or fail closed:
snapshot sufficient state, perform the step, validate model parameters,
optimizer tensors, Gram/retraction residual, and bf16 copy, then commit. On
failure roll back or terminate without saving the contaminated checkpoint.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I4 — Checkpoints do not identify the experiment they resume

**Evidence.** The audited checkpoint payload stores model, optimizer,
scheduler, tracker, step, training config, and guard history. It omits the
activation-store manifest/hash, exact model and tokenizer revision, sites,
source split, whitener and site-renormalization scalars, CLI experiment
configuration, and repository version. The CLI notes that site-renormalization
is not checkpointed. Resume accepts the checkpoint without comparing these
identities. The final report is assembled partly from requested CLI/model
configuration, so it can describe the requested experiment rather than the
loaded model.

**Affected claims/data.** Cross-store or cross-gauge resume can silently
produce a mixed experiment, and a report can misstate effective architecture
or training settings. Resume/checksum drills do not establish scientific
provenance.

**Required fix.** Define a versioned experiment manifest and hash over exact
model/tokenizer revisions, sites, store manifests, transform/renorm, model and
training config, codec contract, and code revision. Embed it in checkpoints;
refuse mismatch by default. Reports must separate `requested_config` from
`effective_loaded_config` and point to the checkpoint/manifest hashes.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I5 — Excluded codec blocks still consume support bits

**Evidence.** [`Codec.fit`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/codec.py) fits the
active-count histogram and block-frequency models before applying the
active-count floor. [`Codec.evaluate`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/codec.py)
removes excluded blocks from the realized mask, but prices that mask with the
pre-exclusion count and Bernoulli models. The probability mass of excluded
blocks therefore remains in the support price.

The defect is directly reproducible using the existing test seed/configuration:
the realized `avg_count` is `0.0`, yet the enumerative support rate is
`4.388147781029806` bits/token and the Bernoulli support rate is
`5.537508070468903` bits/token. The corresponding test says excluded blocks
cost no bits but asserts only count and amplitude, not support rate.

**Affected claims/data.** All Phase-0 codec-derived rate–distortion tables,
frontiers, and the stated 7.8–7.9× rate advantage are **invalid pending
recomputation**. This does not imply the corrected conclusion will reverse;
it means the present numbers do not measure the declared codec.

**Required fix.** Strip excluded blocks before fitting the count histogram and
block-frequency model. For Bernoulli coding, model only the included alphabet
or condition explicitly on inclusion. Assert zero count, support, and
amplitude rate when no blocks survive. Regenerate every codec artifact and
rerun the Phase-0 claim audit.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I6 — The fitted codec is ephemeral

**Evidence.** The codec object has no versioned save/load path. The validation
CLI fits orientation, clipping bounds, support models, inclusion decisions,
and calibration statistics in memory, then writes result metadata rather than
the fitted transform needed to reproduce encoding/decoding. This contradicts
[design.md](design.md), which says the calibration-fitted threshold and codec
are frozen and serialized.

**Affected claims/data.** A reported bit rate cannot be reproduced from the
checkpoint and eval set alone, and no independent decoder can consume the
declared code stream. Re-fitting can change orientation, quantiles, and
support prices.

**Required fix.** Create a versioned codec artifact containing canonical block
rotations, inclusion mask and floor statistics, clipping bounds, quantizer
spec, count model, Bernoulli/enumerative model, calibration mean, inference
threshold, and hashes of model checkpoint, store, whitener, and code version.
Add byte/tensor round-trip and saved-artifact evaluation tests.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I7 — Required scientific endpoints are specifications, not executable gates

**Evidence.** [design.md](design.md) requires, for every BSC, a site-only
encoding to all-site reconstruction FVU matrix, a calibrated-map version,
leave-one-site-out reconstruction, and site-code CCA/Procrustes. It also
requires three effective-span estimators and truncation FVU. The training
report contains basic reconstruction, support, bf16, dead-block, and guard
statistics. [`eval_activation_stats.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/analysis/eval_activation_stats.py)
contains fire/coactivation and decoder/site-energy summaries, not the full
endpoint suite, and there is no run-level route that makes the required
artifacts and gates unavoidable.

**Affected claims/data.** H2's shared-code interpretation and H4's effective
span remain explicitly pending in [findings-phase0.md](findings-phase0.md).
Neither can support a production or Phase-2 claim yet.

**Required fix.** Implement a versioned endpoint artifact and CLI/campaign
route for centered contribution covariance spectra, code covariance, joint
map spectra, truncation FVU, all site-only reconstruction matrices,
leave-one-site-out, CCA, and Procrustes `R²`. Add structural tests and make
winner qualification fail closed when required endpoints are absent.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### I8 — Geometry figures substitute decoder capacity for used geometry

**Evidence.** [`fig_geometry.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/analysis/fig_geometry.py)
plots singular values of raw decoder frames and labels them as effective
dimensions/slowly rotating subspaces. It also uses raw decoder Frobenius share
for site profiles. Those quantities describe available frame capacity. They
do not include code covariance, activation frequency, contribution moments, or
reconstruction truncation. This conflicts with the correct caveat in
[design.md](design.md).

**Affected claims/data.** The committed geometry pages can visually imply
that a block *uses* four dimensions or rotates through depth when only its
decoder capacity was measured. Qualification based on independent probes is
not automatically invalid, but the geometry interpretation is overstated.

**Required fix.** Relabel the existing spectra as decoder capacity. Add
code-anchored contribution spectra, centered covariance, and truncation FVU;
use those for “effective dimension.” Re-render figures and audit captions and
family claims.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

**Required fix.** Commit a corrected, passing, versioned 10k report or mark the

passing artifact. Correct the test comment and, if exact matching is intended,
change the generator or selector rather than the prose.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### M1 — The rank Gram is accumulated at bf16 precision

**Evidence.** Training loss is computed on the bf16 forward copy. In the
audited [`rank_penalty`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/gram.py), the einsum
forms `D Dᵀ` and only then converts its output to float. The multiplication and
accumulation therefore already occurred at bf16 precision, contrary to the
documented fp32 Gram calculation.

**Affected claims/data.** Regularizer values and gradients include bf16 Gram
rounding. The effect may be small, but the claimed implementation is not the
one optimized.

**Required fix.** Cast the decoder block to fp32 before the contraction, or
compute the regularizer from differentiable fp32 masters. Add value/gradient
parity tests against an fp64 reference on ill-conditioned and near-degenerate
blocks.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### M2 — Whitening is an untested scientific intervention, not preprocessing trivia

**Evidence.** The production gauge is shrinkage whitening followed by per-site
RMS renormalization. Fel normalizes inputs to norm approximately `sqrt(d)`;
Anthropic separately normalizes layers; SASA states layer-normalization of
activations. Whitening changes the reconstruction metric, feature covariance,
score ordering, encoder conditioning, and which directions are cheap to fit.
Site renormalization further changes relative site weights.

**Affected claims/data.** The `0.3997` result and all FVU/geometry are valid in
the declared transformed coordinate system. They do not establish equal
quality in raw activation space or direct superiority to paper results under
different gauges.

**Required fix.** Run paired raw/isotropic, per-site layer-normalized,
shrinkage-whitened, and shrinkage-plus-renormalized arms on a screening budget.
Always report transformed and raw-coordinate distortion and at least one
downstream causal/behavioral distortion measure.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### M3 — Production training budget is not yet justified by convergence

**Evidence.** Phase 1 plans two epochs over 38M train tokens, or 76M optimizer
tokens. Phase 0 found that doubling optimizer budget from 12M to 24M improved
FVU by 0.016–0.020, while fresh data changed the clean comparison by only
0.0013; see [findings-phase0.md](findings-phase0.md). The design already allows
escalation to at least 100M if held-out FVU is falling at epoch end.

**Affected claims/data.** The chosen 76M budget is plausible, but not known to
be converged. Architecture rankings can change if arms learn at different
rates.

**Required fix.** Predeclare a learning-curve gate based on held-out FVU slope,
dead fraction, and support stability. Save matched checkpoints for every arm
at common optimizer-token budgets and extend all headline controls—not only
the preferred BSC—to 100M when the gate fires.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### M4 — Harvest identity does not pin the model/tokenizer

**Evidence.** [`harvest_store.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/cli/harvest_store.py)
loads a model alias without an explicit revision. The corpus revision is
pinned, but store metadata records only the model string, not the resolved
model commit, tokenizer revision/files, dependency versions, or repository
commit. The whitener hash covers its tensors and metadata, so it hashes an
unpinned alias rather than immutable upstream content. This falls short of
the transform identity required by [design.md](design.md).

**Affected claims/data.** A store and its transform cannot be proven to derive
from the same exact weights/tokenizer after an upstream alias changes.

**Required fix.** Resolve and pin Hugging Face model and tokenizer commits;
record tokenizer artifact hashes, transformers/torch versions, and repository
commit; include them in store and transform hashes; verify them at every load.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### L1 — Partial calibration needs a hard diagnostic label

**Evidence.** Production requires threshold fitting over the full calibration
split, but [`train_bsc.py`](../block_crosscoder_experiment/cli/train_bsc.py)
allows `--calib-batches` and produces an otherwise normal report. It records
the number of calibration batches but does not make a partial fit
promotion-ineligible.

**Affected claims/data.** A diagnostic run can be mistaken for a production
threshold run.

**Required fix.** Emit `calibration_complete` and the calibration manifest
hash; label partial runs diagnostic in filenames/reports; make promotion and
codec freezing require full-split calibration.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### L2 — Prefetch language overstates residency semantics

**Evidence.** The CLI calls the production setting “prefetch four.” The store
implementation defines a queue depth of four; the producer and current shard
can add residency beyond that queue, and an abandoned iterator can leave its
daemon producer parked until process exit.

**Affected claims/data.** This is operational documentation debt rather than a
scientific result defect, but it matters for host-memory planning and clean
shutdown.

**Required fix.** Call the setting queue depth, document total residency, and
give the iterator an explicit close/cancellation path tested on early exit.

**Implementation status:** [x] Implemented in Phase 0.5; regenerated scientific evidence pending.

### Phase-0.5 campaign incident and recovery

The first normalization harvest exposed an operational shutdown race after
the scalar and layer-normalized stores had completely written their reports.
Both subprocesses exited `-6`: a suspended Hugging Face streaming-Parquet
iterator attempted a range-request retry during Python finalization, then
aborted in `PyGILState_Release`. This was not a normalization-math failure.

`harvest_store.py` now explicitly closes the full owned generator chain after
the finite prefix and registers the same close on exceptional exit.
`verify_store.py` now uses the configured `Whitener.apply` transform (including
token-wise LayerNorm) and compares the raw prefix in bounded-memory chunks.
The two affected 71 GB stores were retained only after every shard checksum
passed and each 100,000-token raw-prefix round trip was bf16 bit-exact. Their
recovery state preserves the original `-6` exit and cites the validation logs;
it does not relabel the first process exit as successful. Valid-completion and
intentional-error smoke subprocesses confirmed clean stream shutdown, and the
post-fix jobe suite passed 263 tests. These are implementation/recovery facts,
not scientific findings from a trained BSC.
